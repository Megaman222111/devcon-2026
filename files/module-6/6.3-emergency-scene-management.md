# Chapter 6.3 — Emergency Scene Management

**Module 6: Emergency Response Procedures for Security Professionals**

---

## Section 1: Your Role in an Emergency

When an emergency occurs, you will be looked upon to **manage the safety and security** of the scene. You must:

- Remain **calm** and **professional**
- Use professional communication skills at all times
- Be **continually observant**
- Lead by **example** — struggling to contain yourself will hinder emergency personnel

You will likely be one of the **first to arrive** or even the one to discover the emergency.

---

## Section 2: Fire

Fire is a common occurrence. As part of regular patrols, be constantly aware of fire hazards.

### Preventing Fire

- Check that **fire equipment** (extinguishers) is in place and exits are functional
- Know the location of **all emergency exits**, including stairwells and exterior fire escapes
- Verify fire escape doors **can be opened** and access is not blocked
- Familiarize yourself with **emergency escape plans**
- Identify individuals who may **need assistance** during evacuation (wheelchair users, hearing/visually impaired)
- **Report hazards** — overloaded plug sockets, uncleared garbage, space heaters near furniture
- Ensure smokers use **ashtrays**; report missing or damaged ones
- Check for **tampering** with fire/smoke alarms
- Note stoves or hotplates **left on** when unattended

### When a Fire Occurs

1. **Activate the fire alarm** immediately
2. Check the area for occupants and **clear all persons**
3. **Close doors and windows** to prevent fire from spreading
4. Follow any additional **post order directives**
5. Only after all persons are safe, assess whether to attempt putting out the fire

### Should You Fight the Fire?

Consider:
- **Size of the fire** — only attempt very small fires
- **Availability of equipment** and your training to use it
- **Presence of dangerous chemicals** — do NOT stay in a room where fire and chemicals coexist
- **Availability of an escape route** if unsuccessful

---

## Section 3: Evacuation Steps

1. **Remain calm** and professional at all times
2. **Do not shout** — speak louder to be heard but avoid anxious yelling
3. Identify the nearest **safe emergency exit**
4. **Instruct persons** on exit location; identify a **meeting place** a safe distance away
5. Direct evacuees to move **swiftly and calmly** — do not run
6. **Arrange evacuation** for individuals with mobility needs

## Section 4: Fire Extinguisher Types

Portable fire extinguishers come in varieties based on fire type:

| Class | Fire Type | Examples |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **A** | Ordinary combustibles | Paper, wood, fabric |
| **B** | Flammable/combustible liquids | Fuel, oil, paint, grease |
| **C** | Electrical fire | Wiring, fuse boxes |
| **D** | Metals | Flammable metals (magnesium, sodium) |

> **You must use an extinguisher rated for the type of fire.** A class BC extinguisher will NOT put out a class A fire.

### The PASS Method

- **P**ULL the pin or remove the safety catch
- **A**IM the nozzle at the base of the fire
- **S**QUEEZE or press the handle
- **S**WEEP the nozzle from side to side

## Section 5: Bomb Threats

Bomb threats are uncommon and often hoaxes, but **every threat must be treated as real and serious**.

### Preparation
- Inquire about your organization's **bomb threat protocol** when starting at a location
- If no protocol exists, rely on **remaining calm** and **thinking quickly**

### Reasons People Make Bomb Threats
- Anger or vindictiveness toward the organization
- Fear — someone with information too afraid to go to police
- Political statement or protest
- Revenge — creating chaos and disruption
- Diversion — drawing attention away from another activity

### If You Receive a Bomb Threat

1. **Remain calm** — gather as much information as possible
2. Note the **exact time** of the call
3. Note the **exact words** used by the caller
4. Try to determine:
   - Where is the bomb?
   - When will it go off?
   - What does it look like?
   - What type of bomb is it?
   - Why was it placed?
5. Listen for **background sounds** that might identify the caller's location
6. Note **characteristics of the caller's voice** (male/female, accent, age, calm/agitated)
7. **Notify your supervisor and police** immediately
8. Follow your organization's **evacuation procedures** if directed
9. **Do not search** for the device unless specifically trained and directed to do so
