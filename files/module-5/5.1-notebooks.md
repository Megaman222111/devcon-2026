# Chapter 5.1 — Notebooks

**Module 5: Documentation and Evidence**

---

## Section 1: Why Keep a Notebook?

You should have your notebook with you **at all times** when on duty. Key reasons:

- Record events and information **at the time they occur**
- Notes are available in the future when your **memory may fade**
- Notes may eventually be **used in court** — they must be accurate, complete, and uncompromised

Always use a **pen** (not pencil) and ensure your writing is **legible**.

---

## Section 2: What to Record — The 5W+H Method

Record details that will help you write reports, recall circumstances, provide information to investigators, and prepare for court testimony.

### Who?
- Who made the complaint? Who are the victims?
- Who are the witnesses? Who are the suspects?
- Who is the owner of the property?
- Record **full name, address, telephone number(s)** for all

### What?
- What happened? What is left to show (damage, evidence)?
- What action was taken? What did you do about it?

### Where?
- Where did it happen? Where is the damage or evidence?
- Where were witnesses? Where is the suspect now?
- Provide **exact address** and location details (e.g., "in the men's washroom" or "the NW corner of the parking lot")

### When?
- When did you hear about the incident? What time did it happen?
- When did you arrive? When did help arrive?

### Why?
- Why did it happen? (e.g., what caused the fight?)
- **Do not assume** — if you do not know and witness information can't explain, write nothing

### How?
- How did you find out? How did this happen?
- How were people behaving? How many people are involved?

---

## Section 3: Types of Information to Record

- Start and end time of shifts, timing of breaks
- Complaints or requests for assistance
- Unusual persons or vehicles loitering
- Breaches of security (unlocked windows/doors, unauthorized access)
- Maintenance issues or safety hazards
- Alarms — intruder, fire, or building systems
- Threats, unusual phone calls
- Indicators of criminal activity

---

## Section 4: Objectivity

Your notes should reflect **facts only**, not your opinions. Being objective means accepting facts and evidence as you find them without drawing your own conclusion.

> **Example:** A witness tells you they "know who did it" based on a rumour. You may write down the name as information gathered, but do not write that this individual committed the act. Share the information with police for investigation.

> **Exception for language:** If quoting someone's words, use quotation marks. Example: Patron — "I'll bring my dog in here if I want and you ain't gonna stop me."

## Section 5: Formatting Your Notes

### Notebook Setup
- Use **employer-provided notebooks** — numbered pages, durable cover
- Use only for **work-related** note-taking (not personal information)
- Record your name at the front, plus start date and notebook number
- When finished, write the end date and keep old notebooks in a **safe place** (you may be called to testify years later)

### Daily Opening Entry
Each shift should begin with:
- The **date**
- The **time** (use 24-hour clock)
- Your **location**
- Name of your **partner** (if applicable)
- **Weather conditions**
- **Special circumstances** as advised by outgoing shift or supervisor

## Section 6: Formatting Rules

- **Fill every line** — blank lines leave room for information to be added after the fact, compromising integrity. Draw a line through any empty space and initial it.
- **Correcting errors** — draw a single line through the incorrect information, initial the change, then record the correct details. **Never** erase or use correction fluid.

### Sample Notebook Page

```
Thurs 7OCT2010
0800 - 1630
Campus centre construction site
Raining, 12°
```
