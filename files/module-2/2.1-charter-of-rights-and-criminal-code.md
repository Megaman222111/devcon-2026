# Chapter 2.1 — Charter of Rights and the Criminal Code of Canada

**Module 2: The Canadian Legal System and Security Professionals**

---

## Section 1: The Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms

The Rights and Freedoms for all Canadians are set out in the 34 sections which form the Charter. Of particular importance to security professionals are the rights defined in **Sections 7 through 14**.

### Key Charter Sections

**Section 7 — Life, liberty and security of person**
Everyone has the right to life, liberty and security of the person and the right not to be deprived thereof except in accordance with the principles of fundamental justice.

**Section 8 — Search or seizure**
Everyone has the right to be secure against unreasonable search or seizure.

**Section 9 — Detention or imprisonment**
Everyone has the right not to be arbitrarily detained or imprisoned.

**Section 10 — Arrest or detention**
Everyone has the right on arrest or detention:
- (a) to be informed promptly of the reasons
- (b) to retain and instruct counsel without delay and to be informed of that right
- (c) to have the validity of the detention determined by way of habeas corpus and to be released if the detention is not lawful

**Section 11 — Proceedings in criminal and penal matters**
Any person charged with an offence has the right:
- (a) to be informed without unreasonable delay of the specific offence
- (b) to be tried within a reasonable time
- (c) not to be compelled to be a witness against themselves
- (d) to be presumed innocent until proven guilty
- (e) not to be denied reasonable bail without just cause
- (f) to trial by jury where maximum punishment is 5+ years imprisonment
- (g) not to be found guilty of an act that wasn't an offence at the time
- (h) not to be tried again if acquitted, or tried/punished again if found guilty
- (i) to benefit of the lesser punishment if sentencing has changed

**Section 12 — Treatment or punishment**
Everyone has the right not to be subjected to any cruel and unusual treatment or punishment.

**Section 13 — Self-incrimination**
A witness who testifies has the right not to have incriminating evidence used against them in other proceedings (except perjury).

**Section 14 — Interpreter**
A party or witness who does not understand the language of proceedings, or who is deaf, has the right to an interpreter.

## Section 2: Sections 7–9 for Security Professionals

As a security professional, you should be especially concerned with Sections 7 through 9, paraphrased as:

- **Section 7:** Everyone has the right to life, freedom, and safety and the right to not have those things taken away
- **Section 8:** Individuals are protected from unreasonable search leading to seizure of their property
- **Section 9:** Everyone has the right not to be held against their will without adequate reason

> You should not view this as a limitation on your ability to do your job, but rather as a **protection**. By familiarizing yourself with permissible behaviour, you limit your vulnerability to legal action and civil suits.

> **You should never confuse your position as a security professional with the powers granted to a police officer.** Your primary duty is to safeguard people and property. For the most part, you should plan to be **observing and reporting** criminal activity, leaving the job of enforcing the law to the police.

## Section 3: Arrest

Arrest is the process of detaining an individual for the purposes of investigating possible involvement in criminal activity. An individual who has been arrested **still has rights**, and you must respect those rights.

### Section 494, Criminal Code — Arrest Without Warrant by Any Person

**494(1) — Arrest without warrant:**
Anyone may arrest without warrant:
- **(a)** a person whom they **find committing** an indictable offence
- **(b)** a person who, on reasonable grounds, they believe has committed a criminal offence **and** is escaping from and freshly pursued by persons with lawful authority to arrest

**494(2) — Arrest by owner or agent of property:**
The owner, person in lawful possession of property, or someone authorized by the owner may arrest without warrant if they find the person committing a **criminal offence** on or in relation to that property and:
- **(a)** they make the arrest at that time, **or**
- **(b)** they make the arrest within a **reasonable time** after the offence and believe on reasonable grounds that it is not feasible for a peace officer to make the arrest

**494(3) — Delivery to peace officer:**
Anyone other than a peace officer who arrests a person without warrant shall **forthwith** deliver the person to a peace officer.

**494(4) — Use of force:**
A person authorized to make an arrest under this section is authorized by law to use force for the purposes of Section 25.

## Section 4: Applying Section 494 — Examples

### Indictable Offence — Found Committing (s. 494(1)(a))

An indictable offence is a **serious offence** (e.g., break and enter, theft over $5,000). To "find someone committing" means you must **catch them in the act** — it is not enough to see the start or end result.

### Assisting in an Arrest While Being Pursued (s. 494(1)(b))

**Reasonable grounds** means facts and information that would lead the average person to conclude a criminal act has occurred.

> **Example:** You hear a burglar alarm, see a police officer pursuing someone running from the building, and the officer yells "STOP, you are under arrest!" Given what you can see and hear, you have reasonable grounds to assist.

### Property Owner/Agent Arrest Authority (s. 494(2))

As a security professional acting on behalf of the property owner, you are authorized to arrest an individual you witness committing a criminal offence on or against the owner's property.

> **Example 1:** You are a loss prevention worker. You witness a person take goods and exit without paying. You may arrest on behalf of the store owner.
>
> **Example 2:** You guard an office building and witness someone steal a laptop in the food court. This is a criminal offence committed on the property.

### Arrest Within Reasonable Time (s. 494(2)(b))

> **Example:** On Monday, you saw a customer steal a $6,000 ring but they escaped. On Wednesday, the same person returns. You MAY make an arrest under s. 494(2)(b) — you witnessed the original offence and are making the arrest in a reasonable amount of time. Always call the police and report the original incident.

> **NOTE:** Any arrest made under s. 494 requires you to deliver the arrested person to a peace officer **forthwith**.

> **You may not make citizen arrests where you personally did not witness the offence.** Example: A patron tells you they think they saw someone shoplift — since you did not see it, s. 494 does not permit you to arrest.

## Section 5: How to Make an Arrest

1. **Identify yourself** — "My name is John. I am the loss prevention worker for Mega Mart"
2. **Tell the person why** — "I am arresting you for theft of a video game"
3. **Check for understanding** — "Do you understand what I have just told you?"
4. **Remove to a private area**, if possible — "Please come with me"
5. **Advise of rights** (OPTIONAL — no legal requirement for security professionals; check with supervisor)
6. **Allow a phone call**, if they choose
7. **Advise they are not obligated to say anything** — "You are not obligated to say anything. If you say something, it may be recorded and used as evidence"
8. **Call the police** and await their arrival

> Record all events and information in your notebook as soon as time permits.

## Section 6: Search and Seizure

A security professional has **no power in law** to search persons or their property.

### Search with Consent

- Common at large venues (concerts, sporting events)
- The person retains the **right to choose not to enter** and therefore not submit to the search
- You have the right to **deny access** if they refuse the search
- If a person changes their mind during the search and asks you to stop, **you must stop immediately**

### Search After Arrest

If you have arrested an individual under s. 494, you **may search** if you have reason to believe they are:
- Carrying a **weapon**
- In possession of **evidence** of the offence
- In possession of items suggesting **means of escape**
- In possession of **stolen property**

> **Best practice:** When possible, do not search — wait for police to arrive and request them to perform the search.

## Section 7: Trespassing and Vandalism

### Trespassing
- Ask trespassers to **leave the premises** first
- If they comply, you've achieved your goal
- If they refuse, **call the police**
- You may arrest if police response will take too long, but consider your options — observe and record until police arrive if no immediate harm

### Vandalism
- Damages to property are occurring — more urgent than simple trespass
- **Call police** and await arrival
- You are within your rights to arrest, but remember vandalism is an act of violence
- Sometimes the best action is to **observe and take detailed notes**

## Section 9: Common Offences for Security Professionals

The most common offences you can expect to encounter:

### 1. Defence of Property (Section 35 CC)

As a security worker authorized by the owner, you may arrest if you believe on reasonable grounds that someone is about to enter, take, damage, or destroy property without permission. The act committed must be **reasonable in the circumstances**.

### 2. Assault (Section 265 CC)

Assault occurs when one individual uses force on another against their wishes. Examples of force include:
- Hitting, punching
- Kicking
- Spitting
- Pushing/shoving
- Grabbing

Assault also includes **threatening** another person or causing them to believe you have the means/intent of carrying out the threat.

### 3. Theft (Section 322 CC)

Theft is committed when someone fraudulently and without right takes or converts anything with intent to deprive the owner.

**Retail Theft:** It is recommended that the offence not be considered complete until the subject has **passed any checkout area and left the store**. Detaining a suspect before they leave allows them to claim they intended to pay.

### 4. Break and Enter (Section 348 CC)

Breaking refers to physically damaging a premises to enter (e.g., smashing a window) or opening something covering an opening (e.g., picking a lock). Penalties include:
- **Dwelling house:** indictable offence, imprisonment for life
- **Non-dwelling:** indictable (up to 10 years) or summary conviction

### 5. Possession of Stolen Property (Section 354 CC)

Possessing property or proceeds knowing they were obtained through a criminal offence punishable by indictment.

### 6. Mischief (Section 430 CC)

Mischief occurs when someone wilfully:
- Destroys or damages property
- Renders property dangerous, useless, inoperative, or ineffective
- Obstructs or interferes with lawful use of property

**Mischief in relation to data** includes destroying, altering, or rendering data useless, or denying access to data.
