# Chapter 7.2 — Identifying Hazards

**Module 7: Health & Safety for Security Professionals**

---

## Section 1: What Is a Hazard?

The Canadian Centre for Occupational Health and Safety (CCOHS) defines a hazard as **"any source of potential damage, harm or adverse health effects on something or someone under certain conditions at work."**

---

## Section 2: Categories of Workplace Hazards

| Type of Hazard | Example | Potential Harm |
|----------------|---------|----------------|
| **Thing** | Scaffolding | Collapse |
| **Substance** | Smoke | Smoke inhalation, death |
| **Material** | Asbestos | Cancer |
| **Energy source** | Electricity | Electric shock |
| **Condition** | Icy surface | Slip and fall |
| **Practice** | Working at height without safety rope | Falling |
| **Process** | Chemical transport | Accidental puncture leading to spillage or explosion |

### Hazards from Uncontrolled Energy Release

- Object falling from height (gravitational energy)
- Out of control chemical reaction (chemical energy)
- Release of pressurized gas or steam (pressure)
- Clothing or hair tangled in moving machinery (kinetic energy)
- Contact with a fallen power line (electrical energy)

---

## Section 3: Identifying Hazards at Your Worksite

When assessing potential hazards, consider:

- What hazards are **present at the site**?
- How will a person **encounter** the hazard?
- How **often** will a person encounter it?
- How will **exposure affect** the individual?
- How **likely** is exposure?
- What is the **severity** of the reaction?
- Is there a way to **safely eliminate** the hazard?

### Common Hazards for Security Professionals

- Isolated conditions
- Traffic (especially parking lots and during traffic control)
- Weather (icy surfaces, cold, excessive heat)
- Angry or uncooperative persons
- Unfinished structures (construction sites)
- Crowds
- Alcohol sales

---

## Section 4: Locating Hazards

To ensure all hazards are identified and located:

- Examine **all aspects of work**, including non-routine activities (maintenance, repair, cleaning)
- Review **accident/incident/near-miss records**
- Study how work is **organized** (experience and age of workers, systems used)
- Consider **foreseeable unusual conditions** (power outages, impact on hazard controls)
- Examine risks to **visitors or the public**
- Assess groups with **different risk levels** (young/inexperienced workers, persons with disabilities, new or expectant mothers)

> Hazard assessment should be **ongoing** — you cannot predict which hazards will remain the same from day to day. A constant and systematic reassessment is a proactive approach to maintaining personal safety.
