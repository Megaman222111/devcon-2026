# Chapter 3.2 — Patrol Skills

**Module 3: Basic Security Procedures**

---

## Section 1: What is Patrolling?

Patrolling is making an **active survey** of the persons or property you are tasked with protecting. When you patrol, you make your presence known — in many cases, the sight of a uniformed guard is sufficient to deter potential troublemakers.

Patrolling is more than just walking around. It involves being **actively aware** of your surroundings and of changes to persons and property. You must be continually observant and mindful of who should — or should not — be present.

---

## Section 2: Be Prepared

Before starting your shift, make sure you are physically and mentally prepared.

### Physical Preparedness
- Are you feeling well? Eat right, exercise regularly, get enough sleep
- Going to work when ill is a hazard to yourself and others
- Ensure your uniform is clean, pressed, and properly mended
- Check that your duty belt items are securely fastened and in working order
- Have ready: keys, notebook, pen, radio, or phone
- Test your radio to ensure it is working

### Mental Preparedness
- Review post orders; discuss with supervisor if required
- Deal with distractions before your shift, or set a time to deal with them after
- Get focused on the task at hand
- Review your assignment — make a mental list of what you must accomplish

---

## Section 3: Arriving at Your Post

Upon arrival:
- **Survey the area** — how will you determine when things have changed if you don't know what they look like to begin with?
- **Familiarize yourself** with persons on site and anticipated arrivals
- Find out about **extra events** (fire drills, site inspections)
- **Develop a pattern** for your patrol duties — once memorized, you can concentrate on actual patrolling

> **Guard against complacency.** Don't assume "everything looks the same as last time, so all is well." Verify each time.

## Section 4: Patrol by Foot

Foot patrol is the most common method and most appropriate for many settings (office buildings, retail, venues, airports).

**Advantages of foot patrol:**
- Not distracted by vehicle operation
- Easier to hear window breaking or smell smoke
- Use all senses effectively
- Access secluded areas (stairwells)
- More opportunity to interact with people

### Safety Points for Foot Patrol

- Use CCTV to see into areas before physically entering
- In parking lots, walk on the **side of the roadway**
- **Do not** use portable stereo systems (MP3 players) — they interfere with hearing
- **Avoid smoking** on duty — affects sense of smell and appearance; at night, a lit cigarette reveals your presence
- At night, avoid creating a **silhouette** near windows and doors
- Walk **quietly** at night so as not to announce your presence

### Carrying Out a Basic Patrol

1. Bring radio, notebook, pen, and equipment
2. Walk **confidently** — be professional; exchange brief greetings but don't become engrossed in lengthy conversations
3. Follow a **consistent route** but avoid becoming so familiar that you stop looking closely
4. **Check doors and windows** — ensure they are locked on each pass
5. Look for **signs something is amiss** — odd behaviour, out-of-place objects
6. In poor lighting, **shine your light** into rooms before entering
7. **Record** anything unusual in your notebook
8. **Contact police** if you find evidence of criminal activity

## Section 5: Patrolling for Loss Prevention

Loss prevention protects saleable goods in retail settings. The amount of protection should match the **value of goods**, ease of access, and **likelihood of theft**.

### Key Points
- Seek clear direction from your supervisor regarding priority merchandise
- Discuss types of goods typically stolen and set up patrols accordingly
- Report frequent theft patterns to your supervisor to help the client make better display decisions

### Surveillance Methods
- Security cameras (CCTV)
- Plain-clothes loss prevention workers

> **Remember:** Theft should not be considered complete until the subject passes **all cashiers and has left the store**.

### Watch For
- Individuals appearing "furtive" — looking around at fellow shoppers or staff
- Looking in the direction of security cameras
- Wearing unusually bulky clothes
- Carrying large bags or backpacks

## Section 6: Patrol Using a Vehicle

Some employers provide a vehicle (car, SUV, bicycle) for patrols at large sites. Advantages include:
- Cover large areas more quickly
- Respond to events faster
- Carry more equipment (first aid kit, etc.)

## Section 7: Controlling Access

You act on the owner's behalf to monitor and control access. You should receive clear instructions as to who is permitted access.

### Key Principles
- **Do not** let individuals persuade you to grant unauthorized access through arguments or emotional pleas
- Note requests and advise the person you'll bring it to your supervisor
- If an individual is persistent, call your supervisor for direction
- If no contact is available, **stand your ground** and deny access
- If they refuse to leave, treat the incident as trespassing

### Access Methods
- Keys, swipe cards, tickets, or ID cards
- Invitations at certain functions
- Name lists of permitted individuals
- Visual recognition in small settings

> **Important:** When checking ID, make sure the photo matches the individual standing before you.

### Controlling Departures
Some organizations require individuals to sign out or return equipment/keys. Know what items to collect and what to do with them.

## Section 8: Surveillance

Surveillance is carrying out patrol duties from a **fixed position**, often with CCTV monitors. Common settings include banks, office towers, retail stores, and hotels.

Use the same processes and safety considerations as standard foot patrol.

### Video Footage from Labour Disputes
- You may be required to gather video footage at labour disputes
- Images taken without consent should be used **only for investigation purposes**
- **Never** release photos or video to media, social networking sites, or the Internet

---

## Section 9: Alarms

Another form of surveillance is monitoring alarms. When assuming your post:
- Find out what **alarm systems** are in place
- Determine the **location of the alarm panel**
- Understand lights, sounds, or other indicators
- Familiarize yourself with **basic panel operation**

Some alarms trigger **automatic requests** for help; others require **manual notification**.

### Alarm Responders
When dispatched to an alarm:
1. **Obey all traffic laws** — you have no special authority to speed
2. Check if doors and windows are **secure**
3. If you see evidence of criminal activity, **notify police immediately**
4. Be continually observant
5. If premises are unsecured, **notify police** for advice
6. Follow protocol for advising the alarm key-holder to reset
7. **Document events** in your notebook

## Section 10: Crowd Control

Groups can quickly become crowds. Places where you might encounter crowds include:
- Sporting events or concerts
- Retail stores (Boxing Day, product releases)
- Demonstrations or protest rallies
- Labour disputes
- Emergency scenes

### Crowd Behaviour Factors
Crowd behaviour is influenced by **emotional status** and **presence of leaders**.

### Strategies for Dealing with a Crowd

**1. Remove the Leaders**
- De-escalate before the situation develops
- Step in quickly to remove individuals encouraging inappropriate behaviour
- Ask them to stop; if non-compliant, advise they must leave

**2. Break the Crowd into Smaller Groups**
- Crowds draw energy from numbers
- Persuade people to wander off or become interested in something else

**3. Seek Help from a Sympathetic Leader**
- A respected leader or community figure may influence the crowd
- Allow them to influence the group; make sure you "have their back"

### Panic Situations
- Crowds will "stampede" trying to escape in emergencies
- Remain **calm and composed** despite chaos
- Address the crowd **confidently and assertively** with enough volume to be heard
- **Do not yell** — it signals loss of control
- Threats and force will only increase fear and panic
