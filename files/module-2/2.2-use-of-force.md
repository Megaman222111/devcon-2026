# Chapter 2.2 — Use of Force

**Module 2: The Canadian Legal System and Security Professionals**

---

## Section 1: The Golden Rule

> **The most important rule about the use of force in your role as a security professional is to avoid it as much as possible.**

> **It cannot be stated often enough: as a security professional, you have no more or no fewer rights than any other citizen.**

Most situations can be handled through **skilled communication**. Escalating an incident to one requiring force is risky for both your **physical well-being** and your **professional reputation**.

---

## Section 2: Section 25 — Protection of Persons Acting Under Authority

Section 25 of the Criminal Code outlines who is permitted to use force and under what circumstances.

> Everyone who is required or authorized by law to do anything in the administration or enforcement of the law — **(a)** as a private person, **(b)** as a peace officer or public officer, **(c)** in aid of a peace officer or public officer, or **(d)** by virtue of his office — is, if he acts on reasonable grounds, justified in doing what he is required or authorized to do and in using **as much force as is necessary** for that purpose.

### When Not Protected (Section 25(3))

A person is **not justified** in using force that is intended or likely to cause **death or grievous bodily harm** unless the person believes on reasonable grounds that it is necessary for:
- **Self-preservation**, or
- The **preservation of anyone under their protection** from death or grievous bodily harm

### When Force May Be Used

In simpler terms, you may use reasonable force when:

- **Aiding a police or peace officer** to stop the flight of a suspected criminal, settle a disturbance, or at the officer's request
- **Protecting yourself or others** from harm or injury (self-defence)
- **Stopping an individual from committing a criminal act** (e.g., pulling back someone crawling through a broken window)

## Section 3: Section 26 — Excessive Force

> **Section 26 CC:** Every one who is authorized by law to use force is **criminally responsible for any excess thereof** according to the nature and quality of the act that constitutes the excess.

You **can be charged with a criminal act** if you use force in an inappropriate way. Even if not criminally charged, you may be:
- Subject to **discipline or dismissal** by your employer
- Charged in a **civil case**

## Section 4: What Is Reasonable Force?

"Reasonable force" is determined by the situation. The amount of force used must be:
- **Consistent** with the physical and mental characteristics of the subject
- **Appropriate** to the situation at hand

### Example 1: Low-Level Response

A small female is witnessed putting cosmetic items in her bag and leaving without paying. When approached, she looks afraid and begins to run.

**Appropriate response:** Grab the suspect by the wrist or elbow to stop her flight. Her fearful demeanor suggests she is more afraid than violent. Her small stature does not require excessive struggle. In a public setting, brute force draws unwanted attention.

### Example 2: Higher-Level Response

You are posted at a large soccer stadium. A fight breaks out between two intoxicated individuals. A police officer directs you to assist.

**Appropriate response:** Apply enough force to pull the individuals apart and restrain one pending further instructions. The individuals are emotionally heated and affected by alcohol — use appropriate restraint to maintain control. **Do not** resort to hitting, kicking, or inappropriate language.

> **Use of force techniques are not covered in this course; it is recommended you take an appropriate course which focuses on use of force and personal safety.**

## Section 5: Section 27 — Force to Prevent Commission of an Offence

> **Section 27 CC:** Everyone is justified in using as much force as is reasonably necessary:
>
> **(a)** to prevent the commission of an offence for which the person might be arrested without warrant, **and** that would likely cause immediate and serious injury to person or property; or
>
> **(b)** to prevent anything that, on reasonable grounds, would be such an offence.
