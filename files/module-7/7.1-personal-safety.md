# Chapter 7.1 — Personal Safety

**Module 7: Health & Safety for Security Professionals**

---

## Section 1: Attitude and Preparedness

Personal safety begins with your own **attitude** and **preparedness**. Use the same method of observing and assessing to determine whether you are fit to perform your duties **before every shift**.

If you are overtired, ill, or otherwise incapacitated, you are:
- A **hazard to yourself**
- A **hazard to the persons** you are supposed to be protecting
- A **liability to your employer**

### Wellness Habits

- Get enough **sleep** to meet your needs
- Eat a **healthy, balanced diet**
- Address medical concerns with a **health professional**
- Participate in activities that build and maintain your **fitness level**

> There is always a physical requirement for security professionals, even at stationary postings. Your fitness contributes to combating fatigue and keeping your observation skills sharp.

---

## Section 2: Safety Equipment and Uniform

- Wear and use all **safety or protective equipment** as required by your employer (steel-toed boots, reflective vests)
- Keep your uniform in **good repair** — torn fabric can catch in equipment or machinery
- Wear a **jacket** in cold weather (prevent hypothermia/frostbite)
- Wear a **water-repellent coat** during rain
- Use **sunscreen** and wear a **hat** for outdoor daytime patrols (winter and summer)

---

## Section 3: Communication and Safety

Develop a habit of **regular communication** with co-workers or your supervisor:

- Check in at the **beginning of your shift**, before/after breaks, and at **shift end**
- This establishes a **benchmark** for your communication patterns
- Your employer is partially responsible for your safety and will appreciate regular check-ins

### Building Relationships on Patrol

Communicate with others during patrols (e.g., stopping at the information desk each time you pass). Benefits:

- Opportunity to ask about **concerns you should know about**
- Those individuals will **notice when you don't stop by**, which may prompt them to check on you

---

## Section 4: Risk Assessment

Consider three broad categories of risk at your work site:

| Low Risk | Medium Risk | High Risk |
|----------|------------|-----------|
| Day shift | Evening shift | Night shift |
| Non-hazardous location (e.g., office lobby) | Parking lots during daylight | Areas with high crime levels |
| | Construction/industrial sites (daytime) | Working around large cash/valuables |
| | Venues with large numbers of persons | Risk of violence (e.g., labour disputes) |
| | | Parking lots in darkness |
| | | Isolated locations |
| | | Areas with limited radio/cell signal |

### Factors to Consider

- **Potential** for a risky event to occur (how likely?)
- **Frequency** of the risk (how often?)
- A medium-risk setting can become high-risk if dangerous events happen regularly

> **Example:** A daytime parking lot in an isolated location where drag racers gather weekly elevates from medium to potentially high risk.
