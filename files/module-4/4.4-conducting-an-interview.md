# Chapter 4.4 — Conducting an Interview

**Module 4: Communication for Security Professionals**

---

## Section 1: What Is an Interview?

An interview is a **respectfully conducted exchange of information** between parties. Your goal is to obtain accurate, bias-free information about an incident.

Before interviewing:
- Have a clear idea of **who you need to speak with**
- Know **what questions** you will ask
- If interviewing multiple persons, interview **one at a time** and ask the **same questions** of each

---

## Section 2: Interview Best Practices

- **Cover your duties** — call for assistance to cover your post if possible
- **Ask permission** to interview — they are under no obligation to speak to you. If they decline, ask for contact information for investigators
- **Move to a quiet location** — fewer distractions improve accuracy
- **Explain the purpose** — tell them you are gathering information and will be recording answers
- **Collect contact information** — name, address, at least one telephone number. Advise it will only be shared with those who require it (e.g., police)
- **Ask one question at a time** — let them finish before asking another
- **Use active listening** — focus on what the interviewee is telling you
- **Keep them focused** — if they go off topic, respectfully redirect
- **Ask if they have questions** at the end
- **Thank them** and ensure they are alright to leave (they may be upset or shocked)

---

## Section 3: Types of Questions

### Open-Ended Questions (for gathering broad information)

Use at the beginning of the interview:
- "Can you please tell me what you saw?"
- "Can you please describe the incident?"
- "Can you please tell me what happened, starting at the beginning?"

### Close-Ended Questions (for specific details)

Use to draw out precise information:
- "What time did you first hear the alarm?"
- "Do you know the name of the person who knocked you down?"
- "Were you able to see which way the prowlers went?"

---

## Section 4: What NOT to Do

- **Do not ask biased questions** that support your opinion about what happened
- **Do not lead the witness** or make suggestions — people may want to give answers they think you're looking for
- **Do not pressure** the witness — if they say there's no more information, stop pressing
- **Do not question their actions** — it is not your place to scold them or judge their behaviour

> Take your time during the interview. Review answers and seek clarification if needed. Frame your questions clearly — if the interviewee appears confused, paraphrase until the meaning is clear.
