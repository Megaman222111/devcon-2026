# Chapter 4.3 — Communicating with Uncooperative Persons

**Module 4: Communication for Security Professionals**

---

## Section 1: Know Your Personal Triggers

Before encountering an uncooperative individual, identify your personal **triggers** — attitudes or behaviours that frustrate you most and cause impatience.

Common triggers include:
- Disrespect
- Sarcasm
- Refusal to take responsibility
- Bullying
- Profanity
- Invading your personal space

> **Plan** for someone to "set off" one of your triggers. Individuals comfortable challenging authority know how to manipulate people and circumstances.

### Recognizing Your Triggers

When you find yourself getting upset, ask:
- What was I doing right before this happened?
- Who was I talking to? What were we discussing?
- Where was I?
- When did this happen?

### Managing Triggers

- End or redirect conversations that would typically set you off
- Change the subject back to the matter at hand
- Ask questions the person should want to answer — this refocuses them and gives you a moment to settle

---

## Section 2: Hostile or Violent Persons

Signs that a situation may be escalating:
- **Increasing volume** of conversation
- **Non-verbal body language** suggesting agitation or anger
- **Threat-making**
- **Inappropriate behaviour** (pounding on doors, trying to remove barriers)

### Response Strategies

- **Remain calm** — do not raise your voice; maintaining composure may calm the upset individual
- **Remove from public area** — ask the individual to come to a less visible location (but be cautious about secluded areas)
- **Offer choices** — people get angry when they feel powerless. Examples:
  - "Would you like the number to call the site supervisor?"
  - "Visitors will be permitted after 8 AM tomorrow; I can get a message to your friend."
- If unresolved, **wait for assistance or police** while monitoring the situation

## Section 3: Intoxicated Persons

### Recognizing Intoxication

- Clumsy, uncoordinated; difficulty maintaining balance
- Mumbling, slurred words
- Bloodshot or watery eyes
- Speaking louder than appropriate
- Dishevelled or unkempt appearance
- Unable to understand or follow direction
- Possession of substance-use materials
- Smell of alcohol on breath (note: diabetic emergencies can also cause this)

### Dealing with Intoxicated Persons

- **Intoxicated persons are unpredictable** — continue monitoring for safety
- **Call for assistance** if they behave in an unruly manner
- **Do not antagonize, ridicule, or harass** — speak calmly, without bias
- **Give short, clear directions** — allow time to respond
- **Do not try to reason** with them — if necessary, call for police intervention

> Remember: your primary duty is to **observe and report**. If you do not need to engage uncooperative persons — don't. But when you must interact, utilize LEAPS, best practices, and professional common sense.
