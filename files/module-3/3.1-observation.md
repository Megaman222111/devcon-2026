# Chapter 3.1 — Observation

**Module 3: Basic Security Procedures**

---

## Section 1: The Importance of Observation

As a security professional, your employer's business is being paid to watch over individuals and assets. When the assignment is passed to you, it becomes your responsibility to safeguard persons and property as though they held great meaning for you.

One of the best skills to develop is being a **competent observer**. Your main goal is to protect persons and property. One way to tell if they have been harmed is to **know what they look like**, or what they should look like — only then can you assess whether any damage or injury has occurred.

---

## Section 2: The Three Steps of Observation

Observation is a series of activities:

1. **Noticing** — picking up information through your senses
2. **Interpreting** — putting the information together to understand what is happening
3. **Recalling** — being able to use the information later

Each step is critical. What would happen if you noticed something, interpreted the information, but could not recall it when needed?

---

## Section 3: Noticing — Using Your Senses

When you notice things, you use multiple senses to process information.

Use your senses to:
- **Continually scan** the area while on patrol
- **Commit details** to memory
- Be watchful for **change**, or things that look different than before

### Sight
- Your primary observation tool

### Sound
- Become familiar with the "normal" sounds at your posting
- Practice being able to tell which direction sound is coming from

### Smell
- Notice smells such as fire, natural gas, gasoline, perfume/cologne

### Touch
- Feel changes in temperature (heat behind a wall may indicate fire; a cold draft could indicate an opened door/window)
- Check doors and windows to see if they are locked
- Take care when searching bags (sharp objects)

### Taste
- **Do not** use your sense of taste to test unknown substances

---

## Section 4: Observing Persons

When observing persons, note key identifying features systematically. Use a consistent approach to describe individuals from head to toe, including:

- General build, height, and weight
- Hair colour and style
- Facial features
- Clothing description
- Distinguishing marks (tattoos, scars, etc.)

## Section 5: Interpretation

Noticing is vital, but alone it is insufficient. You must be able to **apply the information** and respond accordingly.

> **Example:** When you see flames and smell smoke, you determine there is a fire and begin making decisions in response.

Sometimes, your next action may be **no action** — for instance, if everything appears the same as your last patrol, no further action is needed.

### Avoid Jumping to Conclusions

Sometimes information may lead you to assume something incorrectly. Where there could be more than one explanation for behaviour, gather **additional information** through your senses.

> **Reflection:** A man enters an office tower late at night, appearing anxious and in a hurry. Possible explanations include unauthorized access, coercion, or simply forgetting a gift in his desk drawer. A simple question — "Good evening sir, is there something I can help you with tonight?" — can clarify the situation.

### Watch for Distortions

Individuals with strokes or brain injuries may have slurred speech and uncoordinated movement, appearing intoxicated when they are not. When unsure, **gather additional information** while remaining watchful and keeping your safety in mind.

---

## Section 6: Recalling

The final step in good observation is being able to **use the information later**. You need to recall details for your notebook entries and when providing information to police.

### The C-A-R Method

- **Concentrate** — Focus on a particular piece of information and block out everything else (e.g., focus on an engine sound, notice changes in volume or pitch)
- **Associate** — Relate details to something you already know (e.g., "Rob works at the same place as my brother")
- **Repeat** — Practice the information slowly and deliberately; the more you practice, the easier recall becomes
